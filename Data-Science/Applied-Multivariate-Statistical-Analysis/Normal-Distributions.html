In this equation hold two parameters, mean μ \mu μ and standard deviation σ \sigma σ that completely determine the normal distribution. The shape and location of normal curve changes as the mean and standard deviation change.

Changes in Mean (Location)

The mean μ \mu μ of a normal distribution represents the central location of the data. Changing the mean shifts the entire probability density function (PDF) along the horizontal axis without altering its shape or spread. A larger μ \mu μ shifts the distribution to the right. A smaller μ \mu μ shifts the distribution to the left. The overall shape and spread (determined by the standard deviation σ \sigma σ) remain unchanged. The probability of events occurring in a particular range changes.

  • Large σ \sigma σ (High Variance)

    • Data points are more spread out.
    • More variability in the dataset.
    • Higher probability of extreme values (tails become wider).

  • Small σ \sigma σ (Low Variance)

    • Data points are closer to the mean.
    • Less variability in the dataset.
    • The probability mass is concentrated near the mean.

Understanding variance helps in analyzing the consistency of data. Smaller variance means more precise predictions, while larger variance indicates uncertainty.

Unimodal Data Pattern

A unimodal data pattern refers to a dataset where the distribution has only one peak (mode). This means that most of the data is concentrated around a single central value.

Key Characteristics of Unimodal Data:

  1. Single Peak

    • The distribution has only one highest point (mode).
    • This peak represents the most frequent data value or range.

  2. Symmetry or Skewness

    • The distribution can be symmetric (e.g., normal distribution) or skewed (left or right).
    • A symmetric unimodal distribution means that the data is evenly spread around the peak.
    • A skewed unimodal distribution means that data is concentrated on one side, with a longer tail on the other.

  3. Measures of Central Tendency

    • The mean, median, and mode are typically close to each other in a symmetric unimodal distribution.
    • In a skewed distribution, the mean is pulled in the direction of the skew.

Examples of Unimodal Data:

  1. Normal Distribution (Gaussian Curve)

    • A bell-shaped curve where data is symmetrically distributed around the mean.
    • Example: Heights of people, test scores.

  2. Right-Skewed (Positively Skewed) Distribution

    • The peak is on the left, and the tail extends to the right.
    • Example: Income distribution, housing prices.

  3. Left-Skewed (Negatively Skewed) Distribution

    • The peak is on the right, and the tail extends to the left.
    • Example: Age at retirement, time taken to complete a task.
